Source A — Modern Non-Fiction

Source A — Modern travel writing, 2019
In Praise of Slow Travel (Modern travel writing, 2019) There is a particular kind of stillness that belongs only to a train pulling away from a station — a moment when the platform slides back and the world opens, and you realise that the journey has, finally, actually begun. I have been travelling by train across Europe for three weeks, from the grey northern coasts of England through France, into Spain, and back through Italy. I have covered slightly more than four thousand miles. I have not once felt the urge to arrive. This is the peculiar gift of slow travel: the journey is not something to be endured before the destination — it is the destination. On a train, you cannot pretend that the space between places is dead time. The light changes. The landscape shifts from flat plains to the violent geometry of mountains. You eat, sleep, read, talk to strangers, and wake up somewhere entirely different. What we have given up in our enthusiasm for budget airlines is not merely time but texture — the accumulated sensation of distance, of genuinely moving through the world rather than being teleported between airports. We arrive exhausted by airports and call it travel. We do not arrive anywhere; we are deposited.

Source B — 19th Century Text

Source B — Victorian letter, 1847
Letter from a Traveller on the Great Western Railway (Victorian letter, published 1847) To the Editor of The Times, London Sir, — I write to you from the railway carriage itself, having just completed the journey from Bristol to London Paddington in two hours, thirty minutes — a feat which, not ten years since, would have required a full day's jolting discomfort by stage-coach. The sensations of railway travel are difficult to render in prose. One sits in what amounts to a small furnished room, drawn at a speed which the eye cannot reconcile with safety, and yet is perfectly safe. The fields rush past in a green continuity that begins to resemble painting more than landscape. Villages appear and vanish before one has formed an impression of them. Whether this constitutes genuine travel, I am not certain. My father, who journeyed by mail-coach, knew every public house between Bath and London, every stretch of bad road, every ostler by name. He arrived with knowledge accumulated mile by mile. I arrived knowing nothing of the country I had passed through — only that I had passed through it very quickly indeed. Speed, it seems to me, is a form of abstraction. The railway gives us distance; whether it gives us travel is another matter entirely.

📌 What this question asks
What do both writers value about travel — and what do they find unsatisfying? Use both sources.
⏱ Strategy: 8 marks · 10–12 minutes. Use BOTH sources equally. Find connections AND contrasts — don't just describe each separately.
Refer to Source A and Source B. Both writers reflect on the nature of travel. Use evidence from both sources to write a summary of what the writers value — and what they find unsatisfying — about their journeys.

🏗️ Writing Scaffold

Model Answers
Both writers are interested in what makes travel meaningful. In Source A, the writer thinks slow travel is better because "the journey is not something to be endured before the destination — it is the destination." This shows they believe the journey itself has value, not just the place you end up. In Source B, the Victorian writer also thinks about what travel really means. He compares himself to his father, who "arrived with knowledge accumulated mile by mile," suggesting his father's slower journey was more worthwhile. Both writers are unhappy with fast travel. Source A says we "are deposited" at airports, which sounds negative. Source B says speed is "a form of abstraction," meaning it removes the traveller from real experience.
Both writers share a concern that speed in travel removes something important. The modern writer argues that air travel has robbed travellers of "texture — the accumulated sensation of distance", suggesting genuine travel requires gradual, physical movement. Similarly, the Victorian writer implies that the railway fails to provide the "knowledge accumulated mile by mile" that his father gained from slower travel. However, the writers occupy opposite positions. For the modern writer, the train is the solution — the means of recovering what has been lost. For the Victorian writer, the train is the problem. This contrast reveals how perceptions of speed shift across time: one era's revolutionary technology becomes the next era's route to slower, more meaningful experience.

📌 What this question asks
How does the writer of Source A use language to show enthusiasm for slow travel?
⏱ Strategy: 12 marks · 14 minutes. Aim for 3 developed paragraphs. Quote → technique → effect.
How does the writer of Source A use language to convey enthusiasm for slow travel? Comment on: words and phrases; language techniques; the effects created on the reader.
📖 Key Vocabulary
conveyto express or communicate a feeling
vividvery clear and detailed — creates a strong picture
connotationthe feeling a word carries beyond its dictionary meaning
passive voicewhen the subject receives the action, e.g. "we were deposited"
🏗️ Writing Scaffold
1The writer shows enthusiasm through the phrase "..." which suggests...
2The [technique] of "..." creates a sense of... because...
3The writer's choice of the word "..." is effective because it implies...
4This conveys enthusiasm because the reader feels...
Model Answers
The writer shows enthusiasm for slow travel through the phrase "the journey is not something to be endured before the destination — it is the destination." This shows the writer is very positive because they think the journey itself is the best part, not just arriving. The writer also uses a list: "you eat, sleep, read, talk to strangers, and wake up somewhere entirely different." This makes slow travel sound exciting and full of different experiences. The writer criticises flying by saying "we are deposited" at airports. The word "deposited" sounds negative because it treats people like objects, making train travel seem much better by comparison.
The writer conveys enthusiasm through rich sensory contrast. The phrase "the landscape shifts from flat plains to the violent geometry of mountains" juxtaposes the mundane with the dramatic. The adjective "violent" is strikingly unexpected applied to a visual experience — it elevates looking out of a train window into something physically thrilling. The writer's use of the collective "we" — "we are deposited" — implicates readers directly in the habits being criticised. This is a persuasive technique: by making readers feel they have lost something, the writer creates appetite for the recovery of slow travel. The final sentence uses the passive voice deliberately: "we are deposited." Against the active verbs of train travel — "eat, sleep, read, talk" — this passivity reads as a kind of death of experience, reinforcing the writer's enthusiasm for the alternative.

📌 What this question asks
Compare how both writers present their views on travel and the language methods they use. Use BOTH sources throughout.
⏱ Strategy: 16 marks · 16–18 minutes. Compare directly throughout — don't write about one source then the other. Use: Similarly, However, In contrast, Both writers, Whereas.
Refer to the whole of Source A and Source B. Compare how the two writers present their perspectives on travel. Consider: their different attitudes; the language methods they use to convey their perspectives.
📖 Key Vocabulary
juxtaposeplace two contrasting things near each other for effect
registerlevel of formality — formal, informal, conversational
rhetoricallanguage designed to persuade or have a strong effect
ironywhen the reality is very different from what is expected
🏗️ Writing Scaffold
1Both writers share the view that... shown in Source A when... and Source B when...
2However, they differ in that... Source A suggests... whereas Source B argues...
3Source A uses [method] to convey their view: "..." The effect is...
4In contrast, Source B uses [method]: "..." This is different because...
5Overall, both writers are concerned about... but reach [similar/different] conclusions.
Model Answers
Both writers think that fast travel takes away from the experience. Source A says we "are deposited" at airports, which sounds negative. Source B says speed is "a form of abstraction," meaning it removes the traveller from real experience. Both writers agree something important is lost when we travel too quickly. However, the writers have different attitudes. Source A is enthusiastic about trains as a solution to bad modern travel. Source B is less certain — the Victorian writer says he is "not certain" whether the train counts as genuine travel. The language is also different. Source A uses "we" to include the reader and feels personal and passionate. Source B is a formal letter to a newspaper editor and feels more measured and cautious.
Both writers use abstract concepts to express what speed takes from travel. The modern writer mourns "texture — the accumulated sensation of distance" while the Victorian writer laments the loss of "knowledge accumulated mile by mile." The echo of "accumulated" across both texts — separated by over 170 years — suggests an enduring human need for gradual, layered experience that speed continues to frustrate. However, the writers occupy opposite positions on the same technological spectrum. The modern writer celebrates the train as a corrective to dead airport time. The Victorian writer views the train with the same unease the modern writer reserves for aeroplanes. This irony only becomes visible when the two texts are read together. The methods reflect different rhetorical contexts. The modern writer's inclusive "we" implicates readers in collective failure. The Victorian writer's deliberate uncertainty — "I am not certain", "it seems to me" — makes his critique more persuasive through intellectual caution, a tone that carries authority in Victorian print culture.

📌 What this question asks
Write an article arguing for or against: "We are all travelling too fast through life."
⏱ Strategy: 40 marks · 45 minutes: 5 plan + 35 write + 5 check. Content = 24 marks. SPAG = 16 marks.
"We are all travelling too fast through life." Write an article for a broadsheet newspaper arguing for or against this view. (40 marks)
📖 Key Vocabulary
broadsheeta serious newspaper written for educated adult readers
rhetoriclanguage used to persuade — includes repetition, rhetorical questions
tricolona list of three for emphasis: "We rush, we scroll, we forget"
counter-argumentacknowledging the other side before dismissing it — makes your writing stronger
🏗️ Writing Scaffold
1HEADLINE: Write a punchy headline first.
2INTRO: Hook the reader — startling fact, short anecdote or rhetorical question.
3PARA 1: State your clearest reason and support it with evidence or example.
4PARA 2: Second argument — develop with a different point.
5PARA 3: Counter-argument — acknowledge the other view, then dismiss it.
6CONCLUSION: Return to your opening idea. End memorably.
Model Answers
Slow Down: Why Life Is Not a Race Have you ever arrived home at the end of a day and realised you cannot remember a single moment of it? This is what happens when we travel too fast through life. We rush from place to place, task to task, screen to screen. We check our phones over 150 times a day. We eat lunch at our desks without tasting it. We are always busy, but are we actually living? Some people argue that speed is progress. Modern technology allows us to do more in a day than our grandparents managed in a week. This is true. But progress and happiness are not the same thing. Research suggests that people who report the highest happiness spend time in meaningful conversation and creative activity — things that require slowness. It is time to stop. To look up. To notice the world before we arrive at the end of it without ever having really seen it.
The Tyranny of Now Something strange happened at a dinner party recently. Eight people sat around a table, but at any given moment, three of them were somewhere else — in an inbox, a feed, a notification — anywhere but the room they occupied. Nobody mentioned it. It had become normal. We have built a civilisation in which speed is its own virtue. Fast food. Fast fashion. Fast broadband. We have reorganised daily life around the assumption that quicker is better and instantaneous is best. The question nobody asks is: better for what? Speed has real achievements to its name. Medical technology that once demanded months of waiting now delivers results in hours. Information travels in milliseconds. The connected world has driven scientific collaboration, democratised knowledge, and saved lives. It would be absurd to deny this. But speed has costs we rarely measure. When we choose the express checkout, a local business closes. When we read the summary instead of the book, a texture of understanding goes missing that no summary restores. We are not travelling too fast through life because life is moving slowly. We are travelling too fast because we have forgotten that the journey is the point. Slow down. The world will still be there.
